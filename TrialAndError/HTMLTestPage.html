Introduction

Since creating and using mods is a grey area at best, it is hard to find good resources on how to get started. This is especially true for Blender since it was supported way after later than 3DS. So most more experienced / professional mod creators are not using it and most resources are created for 3DS. Things are getting better, but that's the current state.

This tutorial is about creating a simple upscale for The Body. This won't allow you to create new models and custom clothes from scratch, but can serve as a foundation for future work and shows some basic concepts and techniques that I am using to create mods. I will use The Body 2.0 as reference, but everything here works for The Body SE as well, just ignore everything where I talk about the hair mesh.

I am not a 'professional' mod creator, so take everything with a grain of salt, and there are likely way more effective way to do things. These are routines that work for me and I have gathered in roughly a year of creating simple upscales, ports from other games and hair mashups.

Also, I will try to explain things best I can. There will be screenshot sequences via Imgur links attached to the headlines. Shortcuts and keywords will be highlighted with this font. Still, after a year of doing this, it is hard for me to put myself in a complete beginner's shoes. So if you encounter anything that does not make any sense for you, please play around yourselves, research, or, if all that fails, contact me so I can adjust this guide.

This is not a fully-fledged Blender tutorial. I will point out specific things and give general instructions, but I just cannot go into every detail about using and navigating Blender. Lastly, I am not a native, so do not expect perfect English.

General Hints

  • Be patient – Modding is a skill and you will be very slow at the start. Things will get better with practice and knowledge. My first mod took multiple days to be somewhat presentable and looked horrible. Currently I can create a basic upscale in half an hour.
  • Familiarize yourself with Blender and basic 3D concepts – Blender is a tool like any other and at first things will feel cumbersome and you will take a long time to get things done. The more you know about Blender / 3D, the faster you will get and the better your mods will be.
  • Save often and in different files – This one is especially true for the start. At some point you will break your current project you are working on. It happens to everyone. Saving multiple versions will help you to not start from scratch again.
  • Don’t be afraid to try things out – Goes hand in hand with the prior point. Don't be afraid to try things out, a technique you've seen in another tutorial or heard of. Just make sure it is possible to go back and then go wild.
  • Reverse Engineer – Looking at other mods and at vanilla items helps a lot to understand how things work. Import mods from other creators to see what how their models are made, export models and import them using the editor to see how metadata or materials are distributed, etc.
  • Look into Modding Discords and Resources – There are lots of resources out there, sadly a lot of them are hidden in private modding discords, but the TexTools Discord offers lots of help channels and collected resources, same for the Male Modding Discord and probably some other. The search function is especially helpful here, since I guarantee you that someone had a similar problem beforehand.
  • Ask for Help – Lastly, when all else fails, ask for help. We all started somewhere and even experienced mod creators stumble over problems. The community lives from exchanging knowledge.

Your First Mod - Revealing Calfskin Rider’s Jacket

We will create our first mod from the Calfskin Rider's Jacket. I know that revealing mods are not everyones cup of tea, but making this look good will require a certain amount of techniques that are required for most basic upscales of vanilla items. Feel free to pick another item, of you are so inclined.

From TexTools to Blender

Before we start, we have to import the models from TexTools into Blender, to get to work on them. So first of all open TexTools.

  • Navigate in the Item List to the Calfskin Rider’s Jacket
  • Select the Models tab
  • Export FBX
  • If you already have a modded version, press Disable before export, you can enable again at any point
  • Navigate in the Item List to the Emperor’s New Robes
  • Select the Models tab again
  • Export FBX

You can locate the exported models in FBX format in your TexTools folder. To easily open them, press the folder button at the lower right corner of TexTools.

Next up, we open Blender. Here, delete everything you have in the default starting scene. Easiest way to do this, is by pressing A and Del. Next you import your files by pressing File → Import → Import FBX and go to the TexTools folder to import the models you exported. Import both of these files.

After importing both files, your Blender windows should look similar to this:

Note: This will crop up later again, but if you are using Material Preview, you will notice that the textures seem off. This is due to the fact that Blender 3.0 uses Alpha Blend as the default Blending Mode, when Alpha Clip would be correct for models exported via TexTools. Just make sure that every material is setup like this and you are golden.

 

Combining the Meshes

Before we continue, let's hop over to our Scene Collection and see what we have here. Goal of this chapter is to prepare everything for the export later.

  • At the top we have our current Collection.
  • Next we have the Lattices, currently c0101e0279_top and c0101e6116_top. Those store the orientation of the attached children. Same for the objects underneath with Group suffix.
  • Next are the Armatures. These are the bones your mesh is attached to. Currently you should have n_root and n_root.001.
  • Last and most important thing would be the Meshes themselves. Each ends with a Part X.Y.

 

Note: The .001 is automatically added by Blender when you import or add something that would be a duplicate to avoid having things with the same name.

Getting these things sorted is important for later, when we import the edited mesh into TexTools. So let's get it right from the start. Each mesh has to have a distinct Part number, or otherwise it gets ignored. So there must not be, e.g. two meshes ending in Part 0.0.

Let’s take a look at Mesh c0101e6116_top Part 0.1:

  • The 0 before the dot declares that this mesh is part of the mesh group 0. Each mesh group can have exactly one material / texture, so this is important when you later chose if this mesh should use the Skin Material or the Material of the Calfskin Rider’s Jacket.
  • The 1 after the dot just splits up the mesh group into different parts and lets you assign attributes for purposes of visibility, e.g. that this part is the elbow or the wrist and should be hidden when you e.g. wear gloves or something similar.

Let us start cleaning this up;

  • Rename all your meshes according to the group they belong to. Right now we have a Jacket group, a Skin group and, if you are using a 2.0 mod, a Hair group. Each needs a distinct starting number. Let us split this up as follows:
  • 0.x will be the skin group
  • 1.x will be the jacket group
  • 2.x will be the hair group.

You can write really anything you want before Part X.Y, so for ease of use let us rename the meshes as well and not just renumber the parts.

Note: For an easier Import I can recommend to keep the group and the order of the original clothing the same and to adjust the numbering of the skin / hair group. This way, when importing, you don't need to worry about assigning attributes since they can just be used from the original mesh. I did not do that here, since A) I was not smart and B) this is a rather simple mesh. So do as I say, not as I do. ;)

  • Select all of your imported body meshes with Shift.
  • Drag the meshes over to the armature of the clothing you are using with Shift held and drop them. Hold Alt so they do not fall over.
  • If they ‘fall over’, you have to make them right. Go to the orange rectangle (Properties) and set the X orientation to 90 for everything.

Note: Moving your body mesh over to the clothing armature has the advantage that all bones required by the clothing are there. There is also the option in TexTools to always export the whole skeleton, regardless which bones are referred by the model, but this is not the default. If you were to move the clothing mesh over to the body armature, the dangling belts would not have any bones and would not animate correctly.

  • When you moved the body mesh, you can remove the remaining object by using Delete Hierarchy.
  • Now we attach the body mesh to the Jacket armature. A neat shorthand to do this is to select every body mesh part with Shift, select a Jacket part with Ctrl and then use Ctrl + L and select Modifiers. This copies the modifiers (currently only the armature modifier) from the mesh to all selected.

Note: This can be easily done manually as well by selecting the blue wrench, clicking on every part and then selecting the remaining armature as the object.

Note: When you now click on a part of the body mesh and on the blue wrench, you should see that the Armature modifier refers to the remaining armature.

Now we have all our meshes numbered in an orderly fashion and attached to the correct armature, and we can proceed with the fun parts.

Removing Clothing and Resizing

I recommend removing unnecessary clothing before resizing since you can fit the clothes better this way. Goal of this chapter is to remove the sweater and to fit the mesh closely to the body we have.

Removing the Clothing

This workflow is relatively simple and most of the time enough to remove everything you do not want;

  • Enter Edit mode with the jacket meshes selected, you can do this by selecting the meshes in the Scene Collection and entering the Modeling tab.
  • Select a vertice / edge / face of the sweater and press Ctrl + L to Select Linked. This way you select everything that is connected to the selected object.
  • Press Delete and select Faces to delete all selected faces.
  • Repeat until the sweater is completely deleted.

Resizing with Proportional Editing

Next, still in Edit mode I would switch the Viewport Shading to Solid on the upper right corner and switch to Vertex select on the upper left, though that is mostly preference - you can also work with faces or edges. Next, make sure Proportional Editing (PE) is active and select two vertices on the chest. Preferably you want to select the vertices so that everything you do is mirrored, but that's not always possible. Next, we select Move if it is not already selected. Your viewport should look somewhat like this.

If you now try to move something, due to Proportional Editing, you likely move the whole mesh. That is because the radius is very large - move the vertices with the arrows and use your scrolling wheel or the numpad + / - keys to increase and reduce the strength until you only move the part of the mesh you want to move. A grey circle should be visible on your screen that indicates the radius.

I use this method for like 90% of my upscales. There's a few hints I can give here, but in the end, it's mostly moving vertices around until it looks good and fits the body, to be honest, so I cannot give a step by step guide here. You will also later likely do more adjustments on the fly, so keep the general workflow in mind.

Here’s my hints;

  • Hold Alt and press the mousewheel to snap your view into positions like full frontal, side, etc. It is really helpful.
  • Select everything that is connected, most of the time every part of the clothing mesh, before you edit it, so that it is in Edit mode. PE will make sure that everything that is connected stays connected and this saves a whole lot of work down the line.
  • Use the colored arrows and planes to move your vertices and do small increments. This way your mesh doesn't go haywire.
  • Adjust the strength / radius of PE a lot and play around with it.
  • If you want to move two vertices and apart, right click and Push/Pull are very helpful tools to do this. You can e.g. use it to open the jacket even more.
  • If you screw up and vertices that should be connected are not anymore, use the magnet at the top (Snap) and snap to vertices. This way you can reconnect parts that you disconnected. Though this is a bit finicky (and I recommend to deactivate PE for this).
  • If there are parts of the mesh you dont want to warp using PE, select them, right click and Separate - By Selection. Deselect the separated part and include it later again. Keep in mind that this changes the name of the mesh though, so you have to either rename it or rejoin it again (Ctrl + J in Object mode)

After we're done, it could look similar to this;

Note: In Blender, there's the Shrinkwrap Modifier which essentially wraps the mesh around the referred object. This can be really useful for things that are skin-tight and do not have a lot of detail like e.g. tattoos, but since it squishes the mesh together, its uses are limited It's still a useful tool to keep in mind.

Dealing with Clipping

After you are done resizing or removing the clothing, you are done, right? - We, partially. There are two issues you still have to deal with. Clipping, with will be part of the this chapter, and backfaces, which we will do after that.  

Transferring Weights

First, we should adjust how the mesh moves so that it mimics the movements of the body mod more closely so that they move in sync. How a mesh moves is determined by Weights and can be edited manually in the Weight Paint mode. For most simple upscales, you do not need to paint weights manually though, but transfer them and I will not go into details on how do paint weights in this chapter.

I found this at Ani’s Super Quick Blender Tips, just to give credit, and to point out a nice ressource.

The steps are as follows:

  • Go into Object Mode / Layout and select the chest part of the jacket.
  • Go to Modifiers (Blue Wrench) and add a Data Transfer modifier.
  • Select the chest part of the Skin mesh group as your source.
  • Then select Vertex Data, Vertex Groups and Nearest Edge Interpolated.

This would transfer all the weights of the body mesh to the jacket mesh by using the nearest edge as reference point. In our case, we don't really want that. It would mess up the weights in places where there is no chest mesh like the belts and it would mess up the weights on the arms where they connect to the elbow mesh, meaning they would move slightly differently and create gaps (which we could fix in theory, but why bother).

What we want to achieve, especially with body types with large pecs, is that the parts covering the chest moves similar to the pecs we already have. How do we achieve that? It's simple; as vertex group select j_sako_l, duplicate the modifier (done via the arrow) and select j_sako_r for the other modifier. These vertex groups are the clavicles / pectorals and most important for this, and this limits our modifier to only copy these weights. After you're done, Apply the data tranfer modifiers.

Vertex groups refer to the bones of the armature, and if you do some posing via CMTool or Anamnesis you should be already familiar with some, they are just named differently. Sebo is the torso, sako are the clavicles, ude are the arms, hkata the shoulders, to name the most important ones.

If we transfer weights, we should to do some cleanup afterwards. The contraints of TexTools are that the weights a certain vertex should sum up to exactly 1 and that one vertex should only have a weight in 4 vertex groups. On a mesh where we don't transfer weights, that's already the case.

 

But since we did, let's go to Weight Paint.

  • Under Weights select Limit Total which will by default limit the vertex groups to 4.
  • Under Weights select Normalize All which will make sure the weights add up to 1.

TexTools does this as well when importing, but in my opinion its always good to work as clean as possible.

Removing Unseen Skin 

After we transfered our weights, we should remove the parts of the body mesh which will not be seen anyway, in our case the arms, shoulders, back and part of the sides. Skin mesh that is not there, does not clip and does not require resources. Original meshes themselves use only the amount of skin necessary and so do a lot of mods.

  • Let's go back to Object Mode and select the arm and wrist parts of our meshes and remove them. We do not need those in general.
  • Select the remaining Skin and Hair meshes and enter Edit Mode.
  • For better visual guidance, Toggle X-Ray and and set the Viewport Shading to Material Preview. I also recommend using Face Select, but that is more a preference.
  • Now select parts of the mesh that are not visible / should not be visible and delete the faces.

This is also more a thing you have to discover yourself, but I can recommend being rather conservative with what you delete at the start and saving beforehand because adding skin back is always a nuisance. In the end, the result should look somewhat like this;

 

Adding Backfaces

For most materials, the game will only display the textures on parts of the mesh that have their normal vectors pointing towards the camera, other parts will be invisible. - Normal vectors, normals in short, are used to determining the facing of a vertex and also responsible for casting shadows, etc. - Most original clothes have their normals pointing outward and only normals pointing inward where required, meaning, only visible parts. The inner part of the jacket is not visible normally, so it has no normals pointing inward - meaning no backfaces - so it would be see through from certain angles.

You can enable for a material that it should display the textures on the mesh regardless of facing, but this often leads to weird artifacts and I cannot recommend it for most upscales.

You can observe how the textures would be displayed in-game in Blender.

  • Enter Object Mode again, select a part of the jacket mesh and select the Material Properties tab.
  • Scroll down and select Backface Culling and set the Blend Mode to Alpha Clip. (Thanks to Grimm for sharing this trick.)
  • Now, if you don't already have, set your Viewport Shading to Material Preview. If you view your jacket it from the side, it should look somewhat like this;

 

 

Note: You can also do this with the hair mesh to make it more or less invisible, I did that later on.

So, how do we deal with this? There are two techniques I commonly use; flipping and filling. (These are not formal names.)

Flipping

With this technique we just create the backfaces for everything we need. This means, simply put, we select everything we want visible that currently is not, duplicate it and flip the normals to point to the other side. This has the advantage of making the clothing seem more natural and should be used for more open parts of the clothing like our open jacket. The disadvantage is that you have to deal with the skin and possible clipping below because it will also be visible.

  • Enter Edit Mode with the jacket chest mesh selected (also use Material Preview shading here)
  • Select the invisible faces - this is a bit finicky, you can use e.g. with Select More (Ctrl + Numpad +) / Select Linked (Ctrl + L) until you have all of them or use the UV editing tab (more in detail on that later).
  • Select Mesh and Duplicate.
  • Right click and choose Separate - Selection. This has the advantage that we can easily edit our backfaces without having to edit the normal mesh. Keep in mind to give it a proper name.
  • Now switch to the backface mesh in Edit Mode, you can do so by just selecting the dot before it.
  • Remove parts of the backface mesh you do not really need and then select all by pressing A.
  • Open the Normals menu by pressing Alt + N or via the Mesh menu and select Flip. If we now look at the sides, most of the invisible hole should be closed - success!
  • Lastly, you can edit the backface mesh to hide more tedious holes like the one near the chest / shoulders, e.g. by selecting the parts of the sleeve and pulling them together or other means. The backface mesh can clip into your skin mesh, this makes it easier to avoid see-through spaces. Though keep in mind that the outer edges of the backface mesh should not move, otherwise you get gaps!
  • In the end, when looking at it with Material Preview, it should look like this;

Filling

With this technique we 'fill' the gap between skin and clothing or connect parts of the clothing with new faces. This is commonly used for sleeves for instance. This has the advantage that you can remove everything below the newly created surface, making clipping less likely. The disadvantage is that it can look unnatural if the new surface is sticking out too far and, if not existent, you have to find a part of the texture that should be displayed on your surface.

I am using it mostly for sleeves and shoulder holes, so it is not really necessary for our jacket. So make a separate save and try it out, if you want to. Let's say our jacket would not have closed sleeves and delete the existing one.

  • Enter Edit Mode
  • Select the vertices on the seam you want to connect
  • Right Click and Extrude Vertices and merge these together, press M to Merge at Center
  • Select the outer vertices again with Ctrl + L and create new faces by pressing F
  • Split the new faces from the rest of the mesh by pressing Y and merge the vertices together by pressing M - Merge by Distance

Now, as you can see, the sleeve looks all wrong. This is because our freshly created faces do not have the correct UV Mapping. UVs, simply put, describe how the image texture is mapped on the 3D object. We can edit this in UV Editing, and when we enter it, we immediately see the issue. Also, in case you are wondering, the UVs for the game are offset by -1 on the Y axis.

  • Go to UV Editing
  • Select the Diffuse texture of the jacket for better reference (should end in top_a diffuse)
  • Select our newly created faces if you do not have that already
  • Right Click and Unwrap
  • Scale the faces down (you might have to turn PE off)
  • Move the mesh to a part of the texture that should be displayed on this part of the mesh

And we're done! Just make sure the normals point outward, but they normally do. In the end this is not entirely different from flipping the normals, but has other uses and helps to close off holes completely.

Glove Compatibility

Lastly, there's still an issue; if we were to wear long gloves with our current jacket, the jacket mesh would deform and cut into the chest. Why is that? Because it has a Shape Key that is enabled when you don long gloves and still uses the vanilla chest proportions. Orginally, this just squishes the sleeves a bit together that it looks like they go into the long gloves.

If you want to watch it yourself, go to Object Data Properties (Green Triangle), select shp_kat and turn the value up to 1. In theory, you can just delete this shape key and not many would notice, but for completeness sake, I show you how to easily deal with this.

  • With the shp_kat selected, enter Edit Mode.
  • Select everything from the jacket (use X-Ray Mode here) except the outer parts of the sleeve
  • In the Vertex menu select Blend from Shape - below you will now see a small Blend from Shape window which you can expand. Here, just uncheck the Add and it will apply the new Base shape of the mesh to the Shape Key.
  • If we now set the value of the shape key to 1, just the sleeves should be squished together.

Check the Shape Keys of the other mesh parts as well. E.g. our backface mesh has a shape key that you do not need that can be just removed and the arms also have shape keys for shorter gloves. The steps here would be similar, just refer to the Reference Information to see what each shape key does.

From Blender to TexTools

Alright, that's actually it! We are done editing our mesh, so let us export it and import it into TexTools. First, we have to export the FBX, so go to Files - Export FBX. Since we do not have anything extra, we can just leave the things the way they are, though there are cases where e.g. exporting only Selected Objects would make sense or do disable exporting the Animation.

  • Let's open TexTools again and go to the Calfskin Rider's Jacket and the Models tab.
  • When importing a new mesh, Open Editor is the way to go. When you later import smaller changes, you can just import though since assigned materials and attributes are just assigned to the same parts and mesh groups.
  • On the left side you have the groups, on the right side you have the single parts to edit. In my case, since I put the Skin mesh group where the jacket was, I have to change the materials and some attributes.
  • For the Skin group, select the Skin Material, then switch it over to Custom and exchange only the a at the end with a b to use the asymmetric skin material.
  • Check each part of the Skin group. In my case, the neck has the wrist attribute, which means it would be hidden if some item like a glove demands a hidden wrist part. I remove this attribute and add the neck attribute so that it is proper again.
  • The Jacket Mesh requires the Material A - the default material, so that is already correct when importing. Now I have to check each part of the Jacket mesh to make sure it has the correct attribute, e.g. wrist parts are labeled as wrists. Like I wrote earlier, this can be skipped by just not changing the numbering of the jacket mesh and leaving it at 0.
  • Lastly for the Hair mesh, just copy the skin material, set to Custom and exchange the b with a c. Also go through the parts and assign the correct attributes like neck, wrist and elbow.
  • Press Import - and Tada! Now you can test the mod ingame.

 

So that is actually it. At this point you could see some warnings. The one that most often happens should be Mesh: Skin Part 0.0 - Does not have a valid skin element. This will cause animation issues if this is intended to be an animated mesh. which happens when your mesh does not have an armature - just assign it under the Modifier Properties tab. Also, if you forget to export an armature at all, for whatever reason, TexTools tends to revert existing changes, to cover some mistakes I often made.

Hope this helps and you have fun making your own mods. ;)

Other Modding Topics

In this section I will give some hints to more advanced topics that are mostly not inherent to Blender itself, but apply more broadly to modding. I will not go into as nearly as much detail as above.

Increasing the Vertex Count (Subdivision)

It took me a long while to figure out a decent way to increase the vertex count. This is especially useful since the base models have rather few vertices but you want to follow rounded shapes like arms, torso or the body. Instinctively you might think that just using Subdivide does the trick, but by default it causes ugly artifacts. So here’s how to circumvent this issue;

  • Delete all shape keys. Sadly you have to re-add them later, so might not be worth the effort for every mesh.
  • Select all parts of the vanilla mesh, go into Edit Mode and go to Face -> Tris to Quads (Ctrl + J). This is just necessary so our subdivided mesh does not look like shit. ;)
  • Under Modifiers add a Subdivision Surface modifier. Set it up like below;
  • Having these setting should keep your mesh from having ugly artifacts. Increase Levels Viewport as you need.
  • Apply the modifier and edit your mesh. Add shape keys again as necessary.
  • Before you export, go to Face -> Triangulate (Ctrl + T) so TexTools can read your mesh.

Gear Mashups

Making Mashups is actually not that hard. You just have to get the right idea, and then go wild.

  • Import meshes from all items you want to combine into Blender. You can have maximum 4 different materials, including the skin, per item, so keep that in mind.
  • You need to add all meshes to the same armature as described in Combining the Meshes.  
  • Here comes your creative work to combine the items in a manner that looks pleasing.
  • Go to your source item in TexTools and to the Textures tab. Export all the textures and color map.
  • Then go to your destination item in TexTools. Add Material and import all the textures and the color set. Make sure source and destination material are similarly set up. Repeat until you have all required material for your item.
  • Import the mashup you created and assign the correct materials.

Note: There are other ways to point to the correct materials. You can in theory just point directly to the materials of other items via the Custom Material by refering to the ID. You can also use the Copy / Paste Material workflow from TexTools. This has the side effect that when the textures in the source items change, the textures for your mashup change as well. This way your mashup mod is completely independent from the source items.

Hair Mods

  • When doing hair mashups you pretty much have to do similar steps to when you do Gear Mashups. They are pretty straight forward for the most part.
  • When working with hair, I recommend importing the head of the race you want to port this hair to, so you can see if the hair matches to the head. You can also use the head mesh to Transfer Weights to the new hair in case you port hair to a different race. Neck movement is the tricky part of this, so beware of clipping here.
  • Hats are rather simple. You have two tools here at your disposal. To check how you hair would look with with a hat, just import one. I recommend the Calfskin Rider’s Cap since it is not that large.
  • For one you can assign the Attribute Scalp on Import to hide a part of your mesh when a hat is worn.
  • Secondly, you can add a Shape Key called shp_hib to adjust the shape of the hair when you wear a hat, i.e. move some strands closer to the head.
  • Physics are a bit tricky. You cannot have arbitrary bones in a hairstyle, but you have to use the bones that are part of the hairstyle with the exact same name. So to have mostly natural physics you should use a hairstyle that is similar in shape to the hairstyle you want to add. If the hair already has physics, you can simply rename the vertex groups to match the new bones. Do not worry about how physics interact with hats, they are disabled when you wear one.

Ports

  • So, for ports you require the following things;
  • A mesh you can import into Blender.
  • For Gear you usually need normal, diffuse and specular textures, though normal and diffuse, or even a diffuse texture can be enough (see below).
  • For Hair you usually need normal and diffuse textures, though a diffuse texture can be enough.

Note: There are multiple ways to create textures. You can use e.g. GIMP or Photoshop filters to create Normal maps from a diffuse texture, though they are obviously not as high quality as you want.

  • You can get these things easiest via tools like umodel for games that use the Unreal Engine like Lost Ark or Blade and Soul, to name popular examples. I have only experience with this tool, so here are my experiences.
  • To find out how this works and which versions you require for which game, use Google. This is a whole nother rabbit hole I won’t go into.
  • umodel exports so called PSK files. You require this plugin to import them into Blender.
  • You need to rotate and scale the models to match the vanilla models.
  • The models have slightly different ‘idle’ poses. You can use the pose mode to adjust the bones. I have noticed that 15° for the upper arms and 7.5° for the lower arms works quite well. Then apply the armature modifier so that the new pose is applied to the mesh.
  • Meshes from umodel are slightly ‘blocky’. Use Shade Smooth to smooth it out.
  • If you mesh has multiple materials, you can use the Material Tab and Select to easily separate the mesh groups for the materials. You can also use the Material Tab to apply e.g. the diffuse textures so you can easier work with the model while seeing what each part actually is.
  • You need to open the exported texture files and save them as DDS / PNG again, since TexTools cannot parse the exported files from umodel.
  • Most of the time you need to apply the Alpha channel from the Diffuse texture to the Blue channel from the Normal texture so that the transparent parts are also transparent ingame.
  • When you have your mesh imported and the form is correct, the easiest way to get a somewhat decent result is to use an item that has a similar shape from the game as reference and copy the weights, and adjust things after. For this you have to delete all current vertex groups, create a Data Transfer modifier and press Generate Data Layer before applying. This way all your vertex groups are setup and transfered correctly. Afterwards its finetuning time, so most of the work. You will likely also need to adjust the weights manually quite a bit!

Note: You can also keep the weights and reassign them by mapping the upper arm bones to the ingame equivalents. Though in the end, I do not think that this saves time, but just so you are aware.

  • You need to setup a new Material and import the textures. For a simple port I recommend using the Gear - Diffuse Specular Shader. Set the last colorset row to White / White and Gloss 20 and set it to undyeable. Then assign your material on import.
  • If you want your port to be fully dyeable, you need to put in a lot of work. You need to use the Default gear shader, create a mask texture from the diffuse map, make the whole normal map colorset compatible and then assign the colors via the color set. It is rather exhausting and for most finely made gear ather finicky, so I only recommend it if you are patient and frustration resistant. ;)
  • To see what each channel of each texture does, use the TexTool Reference.

Note: The different between default and diffuse specular shading for gear is as follows; by default ingame gear has no ‘colors’, just normals and a mask, and assigns colors and materials via the Colorset. This way you can color your gear arbitrarily. Diffuse textures work different. The gear then has a base color in form of the diffuse map. Colors you would apply via dye are then applied multiplicatively to the base color. In short, when you have a diffuse that doesn’t have white base colors, better to

make it undyeable and white.

  • Lastly, to make your port work with shoes or gloves (or hats in case of hair), you need to do the usual like splitting up the meshes (always you Split before Separating!), assigning attributes and shape keys.
